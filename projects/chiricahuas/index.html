Fauntastic Chiricahua Mountains

Skills used: QGIS, open sourced software, self-led project

In the summer of 2016, I completed a three month internship at the Southwest Research Station, operated by the Smithsonian Museum, in the Chiricahua Mountains of southeastern Arizona. I found one of the most fascinating places I had ever been to. This mountain range, which juts suddenly out of the surrounding Sonoran Desert, creates a so-called "sky island" which attracts animals from several of the surrounding ecoregions together into one of the most biodiverse places in the contiguous United States. I decided to create a map to show just how special this place is, especially for avid birders for whom the spot is often near the top of their "must go" list. I wanted to experiment with different mapping software, and I chose QGIS to create this map as I am a supporter of their open-source model. The data used is compiled from confirmed species observations made on the iNaturalist platform, which is widely used by both visitors to and inhabitants of the mountains, many of whom are retired professors. About Me

My name is Ian Maher. I am currently working as a GIS Technician with 3J Consulting, a Portland, Oregon based engineering and urban planning firm. I love making maps and analyzing spatial data. I believe all aspects of bringing spatial data to people, including cartography, application development, and communication, are all important aspects of what makes GIS special.

My diverse interests include scuba diving, coding, making music, hiking, reading, and traveling. Each of these pursuits has shaped my perspective and approach to geographic information systems and data visualization.
